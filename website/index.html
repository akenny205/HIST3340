01

Introduction

What does it mean for a digital document to be treated as a piece of text?

The PDF shares many qualities with printed texts, manuscripts, and other textual pieces. Yet it exists in a fundamentally different realm, one of electricity, software, and instant duplicability.

This project investigates how the digital world shapes our understanding of what a "text" is, examining how the PDF is similar to and different from traditional pieces of text in how it is read, owned, and preserved.

Ownership & Access

Do PDFs maintain the same sense of ownership that print texts do? The answer is complicated.

üìö Owning a Book

  • Physical possession
  • Can lend, sell, or gift
  • Degrades over time
  • One copy per purchase
  • Marginalia becomes part of the object

üìÑ Owning a PDF

  • File on a device
  • Infinitely copyable
  • Doesn't degrade (but formats change)
  • Often licensed, not owned
  • Annotations may not persist

Consider:

Developments in the creation of texts and their digitization have shaped who has access to texts along with information throughout history. The labor involved in producing texts has changed dramatically over time.

05

Conclusion

Digital texts represent new configurations of textual technology and experience.

The PDF's materiality, its ease of creation, deletion, and sharing, along with features like hyperlinks, pagination, and compression, offers a sharp contrast to the invention and evolution of print materials.

Rather than viewing digital texts as simple replacements for print, we might better understand them as artifacts with their own generative processes and histories.

Deletability

Digital files can be deleted with a keystroke, but "deletion" is more complicated than it appears. Traces remain in ways that physical destruction does not allow.

The Illusion of Deletion

When you delete a PDF, the file doesn't actually disappear. The operating system simply marks that disk space as "available", the data remains until it's overwritten by something else. This creates a paradox: digital files are both easier to delete and harder to truly destroy than physical texts.

Where Traces Remain

A "deleted" PDF may persist in multiple locations:

  • Disk sectors , Until overwritten
  • Email servers , If ever sent as attachment
  • Cloud backups , Automatic syncing
  • Recipient copies , Once shared, uncontrollable
  • Web archives , Wayback Machine, etc.
  • Forensic recovery , Professional tools

Historical Implications

Book burning has been used throughout history to suppress ideas. But digital texts resist this kind of destruction, they proliferate too easily. Conversely, digital texts face new threats: format obsolescence, platform shutdown, and the fragility of storage media.

The Permanence Paradox

Digital texts exist in a strange state: individually fragile (a corrupted bit can render a file unreadable), yet collectively persistent (copies spread faster than they can be suppressed). A burned book is gone forever; a "deleted" PDF may resurface years later. This changes the nature of censorship, privacy, and historical preservation.

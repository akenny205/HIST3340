Font

Type is never neutral. Every letterform carries history, culture, and meaning, choices made by designers, printers, and writers that shape how we receive the words on the page.

The Weight of Metal Type

Before digital fonts, type was physical. Each letter was cast in metal, stored in cases, and assembled by hand into a composing stick. The compositor selected each character, placed it in sequence, and locked it into a chase for printing. This labor, measured in hours, not milliseconds, meant that typographical choices had material consequences.

Typography as Rhetoric

Marcy Dinius's analysis of David Walker's Appeal (1829) reveals how typography itself can be a radical act. Walker's text explodes with exclamation points, italics, capitals, and varied type sizes, not as decoration but as argument. "Look!! Look!!! at This!!!!" demands the reader's attention in ways that uniform text cannot. The typography performs the urgency of the message.

Typefaces as Stereotype

Rob Giampietro's "New Blackface" examines how typefaces like Neuland and Lithos became visual shorthand for African and African American culture, used on everything from movie posters to restaurant menus. These "stereotypographic" choices encode racial assumptions into letterforms. Typography speaks before we read the words, and what it says is not always innocent.

East and West

Kai-wing Chow's "Reinventing Gutenberg" reminds us that moveable type was invented in China and Korea centuries before Gutenberg. But while European type enabled a printing revolution, Chinese woodblock printing remained dominant, not due to technological inferiority, but because it better suited the logographic writing system and the cultural contexts of textual production.

Fonts in PDFs

A PDF can embed fonts or rely on system substitution. Embedded fonts preserve the designer's intent; substituted fonts replace the original with whatever is available. The same document can look different on different machines, a reminder that even digital typography depends on material conditions.

Course Connections

In the printing workshop, we set type by hand, selecting each letter from the case, placing it in the composing stick, and experiencing the physical labor that once produced every printed page. The slowness is instructive: it reveals why typographical choices mattered so much when changing them meant hours of work.

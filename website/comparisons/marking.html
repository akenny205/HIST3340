Ink

Ink transforms thought into permanent mark. Once pressed into paper fibers, it becomes part of the material record, evidence of a moment, a hand, a decision that cannot be undone.

The Chemistry of Permanence

Medieval scribes mixed iron gall ink from oak galls, iron sulfate, and gum arabic, a formula that bonded chemically with parchment to create marks that would last centuries. As the Getty Museum's "Making Manuscripts" demonstrates, creating ink was itself a craft requiring knowledge of materials and technique.

Gutenberg's Revolution

As Stephen Fry explores in "The Machine That Made Us," Gutenberg's printing press required not just movable type but a new kind of ink. Water-based inks used by scribes wouldn't adhere to metal type. Gutenberg developed oil-based ink, a material innovation as important as the press itself. Without the right ink, there would be no printing revolution.

Ink as Evidence

Because ink is physical, it can be analyzed. Forensic examination of manuscripts reveals forgeries, dates compositions, and traces the movement of texts across time and space. The iron content, the particle size, the chemical signature, all tell stories the writer never intended to leave. Digital text leaves no such material trace.

The Meaningful Mark

As Marcy Dinius argues in her analysis of David Walker's Appeal, typography itself carries meaning. Walker's radical use of exclamation points, italics, and varied type sizes wasn't decorative, it was rhetorical, demanding the reader's attention in ways that the uniform pixels of a screen cannot replicate. The weight of ink on paper, the pressure of impression, the darkness of the mark, these are choices with consequences.

Course Connections

Rob Giampietro's "New Blackface" examines how typefaces like Neuland and Lithos became visual shorthand for racial stereotypes, a reminder that the marks we make carry cultural meaning beyond their semantic content. Typography is never neutral; it speaks before we read the words.

In a PDF, text is rendered by software interpreting code. The "ink" is light from a screen, and every copy is identical. There is no hand, no pressure, no variation, and no forensic trail connecting document to maker. The mark loses its status as evidence.
